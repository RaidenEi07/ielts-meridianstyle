The Return of the Urban Beekeeper

Across the rooftops of Europe's largest cities, a quiet transformation is taking place. Where once there were only chimneys and aerials, today there are beehives — tens of thousands of them. Urban beekeeping, long dismissed as an eccentric hobby, has become a genuine movement, embraced by restaurants, hotels and even financial institutions keen to demonstrate their environmental credentials.

The reasons for this revival are varied. Some keepers are drawn by the promise of honey with a distinctive local character; city bees, it turns out, often produce a more diverse harvest than their rural counterparts, foraging as they do across parks, gardens and railway embankments. Others are motivated by a sense of stewardship, believing that cities can offer bees a refuge from the pesticides that dominate modern agriculture.

Yet the trend is not without its critics. A growing number of ecologists warn that the enthusiasm for honeybees may be misplaced. Honeybees are, after all, a managed species — effectively livestock — and an excessive concentration of hives can place strain on the limited floral resources available, crowding out the wild bees and other pollinators that are, in many respects, more important to urban ecosystems.

This tension has prompted some cities to introduce guidance on hive density, and to encourage would-be keepers to plant for pollinators before acquiring a colony. The most thoughtful practitioners now speak less about honey yields and more about habitat — about creating the conditions in which all pollinators, managed and wild alike, might thrive together.

LondonParisBerlinMadrid
